About F.O.R.G.E

What Is F.O.R.G.E?

F.O.R.G.E (Framework for Organized, Resilient, Governed Engineering and Development of Agentic AI) is a structured methodology for building, governing, and scaling AI agent systems. It provides a taxonomy of {{TOTAL_TECHNIQUES}} techniques organized across {{TOTAL_TACTICS}} tactical pillars, giving teams a common vocabulary and proven practices for agentic AI development at every level of application.

Modeled after frameworks like MITRE ATT&CK, F.O.R.G.E organizes practitioner knowledge into an interactive matrix where each technique represents a specific, actionable practice that teams can adopt, adapt, and measure.

Why F.O.R.G.E Exists

Agentic AI development is no longer experimental. Teams across every industry are deploying AI agents into production workflows. But there is no standardized framework for how to build, govern, and scale these systems effectively. Academic frameworks exist but are theoretical. Vendor documentation covers tools, not methodology.

F.O.R.G.E fills this gap. It captures operational knowledge from teams that have built production agentic AI systems, organized into a navigable, referenceable structure that any team can use - from solo practitioners to enterprise organizations.

Origin

F.O.R.G.E was developed by Pete McKernan and his agentic AI development team, a collaboration that produced 463,000 words of documentation, a patent filing, multiple software products, and a comprehensive governance system in its first two weeks of operation. The methodology draws from 20 years of military command, red team operations, QA engineering, and game design.

The team's own operations serve as the reference implementation of F.O.R.G.E. The framework itself is designed to be universal - applicable to any agentic AI system at any scale.

Framework Structure

F.O.R.G.E uses a matrix structure inspired by MITRE ATT&CK:

  • Tactics (columns) represent the strategic pillars of agentic AI development
  • Techniques (cells) represent specific practices within each tactic
  • Each technique includes a description, implementation guide, success indicators, failure modes, and a field report from real-world use
  • Techniques are identified by ID (e.g., FG-0101) for precise reference
